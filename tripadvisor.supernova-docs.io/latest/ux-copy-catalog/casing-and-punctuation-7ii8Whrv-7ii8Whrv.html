Casing & punctuation

Quick tips on casing

Most common uses

Headlines: sentence case, no punctuation, unless there are multiple sentences, or used for emphasis.
Subheads: sentence case, punctuation like a normal phrase or paragraph.

Example:
Reviews summary 
This summary was created by AI, based on recent reviews.

Shelves

Shelves are sentence case, and mostly don’t have end punctuation unless there are two sentences. This goes for headlines and subheads.

Example:
Score big deals on New York City hotels 
Compare 943 through photos and reviews. Fully refundable options available.

CTAs

CTAs are always sentence case and do not get punctuation. This also goes for CTAs at the end of shelves or disclaimers, like Learn more or See terms.

Example:
Traveler’s Choice: traveler faves in New York City 
Based on reviews and ratings, with Best of the Best awarding the top 1%. Learn more

Casing for TA Products

Our core products

Tripadvisor (the brand) and all products that we offer should be written in Title Case. 

  • Tripadvisor 
  • Tripadvisor for Business 
  • Tripadvisor Ads (aka Ads) 
  • Management Center 
  • Premium 
  • Sponsored Placements 
  • Business Advantage 
  • Travelers’ Choice

Special case: Trips

Trips is capitalized when speaking about the product at the highest level.

Example: Collaborate on your next girls weekend with Trips.

When describing how users save items on our site, or when referring to an excursion, trips is lowercase.

Example: Find inspiration for your girls trip to Paris. 
Example: Save this hotel to a trip so you don’t forget it.

Special case: Things to Do

Things to Do should be written in Title Case when speaking about the overall category (most often in CTAs) or as a menu item.

Example: Explore Things to Do in New York City.  

Write things to do in sentence case when talking about a general assortment of experiences or activities.

Example: If you love art, you’ll find plenty of things to do in NYC.

Punctuation

End punctuation

  • Don’t use end punctuation in headlines (unless it’s for emphasis).
  • Don’t use end punctuation in shelf heds.
  • Don’t use end punctuation in shelf sublines, but…  
  • DO use end punctuation on shelf sublines IF they’re multiple sentences.

Periods

  • No periods with an abbreviation of solid compound words. Ex: NW (northwest) 
  • No periods with acronyms.  Ex: NATO 
  • No periods with metric abbreviations. Ex: 6 mm 
  • No periods with shortened word forms. Ex: ad (advertisement)

Commas

Use the Oxford comma before “and” but never when using an ampersand.

  • Yes: first item, second item, and third item 
  • Yes: first item, second item & third item 
  • No: first, second and third 
  • No: first, second, & third

And vs. Ampersands

Default to “and” (spelled out), but other headers and site copy can use “&” more liberally to save space (limit use of “+” to totals/numerical values). Subheads should always use “and.” 

Spacing

Zero spaces required on either side of an ellipsis…like this.

Parentheses

If the parentheses fall within a sentence, and are not a full sentence on their own, do not capitalize what’s inside and keep the period on the outside.

Example: This hotel is just right for a big night out (small ones, too).

Quotation Marks

Punctuation should go inside the quotation marks, always, no exceptions.

Example: “I go to Amsterdam when I’m tired of everywhere else,” Nicole said. Example: They’re getting tired of hearing people call NYC “The Big Apple.”
